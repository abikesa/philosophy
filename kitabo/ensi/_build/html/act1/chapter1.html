import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def draw_triangle(ax, vertices, labels, color='black'):
    """Draws a triangle given vertices and labels for each vertex with matching color."""
    triangle = plt.Polygon(vertices, edgecolor=color, fill=None, linewidth=1.5)
    ax.add_patch(triangle)
    for i, (x, y) in enumerate(vertices):
        ax.text(x, y, labels[i], fontsize=12, ha='center', va='center', color=color)  # Set label color

def get_triangle_vertices(center, radius):
    """Returns the vertices of an equilateral triangle centered at `center` with a given `radius`."""
    angles = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 4)[:-1] + np.pi/2  # angles for vertices of an equilateral triangle
    return np.column_stack([center[0] + radius * np.cos(angles),
                            center[1] + radius * np.sin(angles)])

Adam Smith’s The Theory of Moral Sentiments can be understood as having a fractal structure, where key ideas repeat and evolve in a layered way. The first fractal has three main parts. The first part relates to strategy, and in Smith’s framework, the essence of strategy is duty to society. Duty represents the moral obligations individuals must consider in their interactions with others. It’s a foundational aspect of moral reasoning, setting the course for decisions and guiding behavior in a social context.

The second part of the fractal concerns payoffs to oneself, which can be understood as reward and punishment. Smith emphasizes that human behavior is influenced by the social feedback one receives, whether through praise, blame, or other forms of recognition. This dynamic of reward and punishment shapes our moral compass, as individuals adjust their actions based on the anticipated reactions from others.

The third part deals with equilibrium in Paradiso, which is fundamentally about action. For Smith, morality is ultimately expressed through action—whether those actions are appropriate or inappropriate within the given context. Thus, moral judgment is always tied to the decisions we make and the behaviors we exhibit in society. Action is where moral theory is put into practice.

The second iteration of this fractal occurs in parts 4, 5, and 6, where the same structure repeats with more complexity. Here, the strategy shifts to custom, reflecting how societal norms and traditions shape moral duties.

Payoffs in this context are framed as utility, emphasizing the practical benefits or harms that result from moral choices.

Finally, the equilibrium in this second layer is represented by systems of moral philosophy, which formalize and rationalize the interactions between duty, custom, reward, and action. This recursive structure, where each part contains subsections and chapters, gives Smith’s work its depth and allows for a more comprehensive exploration of human morality. It’s through this fractal approach that The Theory of Moral Sentiments systematically addresses how individuals and societies navigate the complexities of moral life.

Mixed metaphors in literature, especially in sacred texts like Psalms 23, often reflect an amalgamation of ideas that aim to evoke multiple layers of meaning. However, the effectiveness of these metaphors can vary depending on how seamlessly they are integrated. When they collide awkwardly, as in your critique that pivots to comparing divine guidance to the pampered life of Kobe beef, the dissonance becomes glaring. This contrast highlights the inherent risk in mixing metaphors: the potential trivialization of a profound or solemn message through inappropriate or jarring imagery.

Rod and Staff: Symbols of Guidance and Protection#

In Psalms 23, the rod and staff symbolize guidance and protection. The shepherd, who is both a caretaker and leader, uses these tools to ensure the sheep’s well-being and safety. The metaphor draws upon the imagery of shepherding, which would have resonated deeply with ancient pastoral cultures. It conjures feelings of trust, comfort, and safety under the watchful eye of a protector.

However, the mixed metaphor that likens divine care to the pampering of Kobe beef introduces a jarring comparison. Kobe beef is celebrated for its luxurious treatment, where the cattle are massaged and fed a specialized diet to ensure a tender, high-quality product. The comparison implies that humans, under divine care, are coddled and prepared for an end that could be interpreted cynically: being “fattened up” for some ultimate sacrifice or consumption.

The Dangers of Mixing Metaphors#

The critique here lies in how this mixed metaphor may undermine the sacred nature of the original text. The pastoral and spiritual language of Psalms 23 emphasizes nourishment of the soul, moral guidance, and resilience in the face of adversity. Introducing imagery of Kobe beef, a commercial product valued for its eventual consumption, shifts the focus from spiritual elevation to material indulgence. It trivializes the comfort derived from divine protection by reducing it to mere physical pampering.

Moreover, “dwelling in the house of the Lord forever” is a metaphor for eternal peace and spiritual fulfillment. When juxtaposed with the image of Kobe beef, it risks appearing like a passive acceptance of a spoiled, sedentary existence—leading to the question of what kind of existence that entails. Are we spiritually elevated beings in the divine presence, or are we simply led to a life of indulgence without agency, fattened for some unstated purpose?

Why the Metaphor Collapses#

This collapse of metaphors speaks to a larger issue: when metaphors aren’t aligned in tone or message, they risk distorting the original meaning. Psalms 23 works because its pastoral metaphors operate within a consistent framework of spiritual care and guidance. The Kobe beef metaphor, however, introduces a modern, consumerist lens that reduces the sacred act of divine protection to a commodified process. This is an irreconcilable shift in tone.

Fleshing Out the Critique#

To flesh out the critique, you could further explore how mixed metaphors disrupt narrative flow. By inserting consumerist imagery into a religious context, you risk alienating the reader from the original spiritual intent. The metaphor loses its ability to resonate on a deep, emotional level, instead becoming a caricature of the original message. Kobe beef’s pampering leads to death, not enlightenment or salvation—a notion antithetical to the redemptive arc of Psalms 23.

In literature, mixed metaphors often fail because they combine elements that evoke conflicting emotional responses. The green pastures and still waters symbolize peace, renewal, and spiritual contentment. Kobe beef, in contrast, represents luxury, indulgence, and ultimately, consumption. The shift from spiritual nourishment to physical pampering breaks the metaphor’s coherence and can undermine the sacred message.

In conclusion, mixed metaphors should be wielded with care, as their power lies in their ability to enhance meaning, not dilute it.
